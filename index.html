Enterprise AI Transformation Arrives: Liatrio Deploys Claude Across Aerospace and Healthcare
AI adoption accelerates beyond predictions — engineers report they "didn't write a single line of code"

Something is changing in the halls of America's most complex engineering organizations. At Boeing Digital Services and Natera Inc., two of the country's most demanding technical environments, a quiet revolution is underway — and it is being driven by a single model: Claude.

Liatrio, the enterprise engineering transformation firm, has spent the past several months embedding Claude directly into the daily workflows of hundreds of engineers across aerospace, defense, and FDA-regulated clinical software. The results have defied even the most optimistic projections.

"We're not doing pilots," said one Liatrio engagement lead. "We're doing production. Claude is in the repo, in the review pipeline, in the deployment workflow. It's not a tool engineers open once a week. It's the way they work now."

At Boeing Digital Services, a provisioning workflow that previously consumed five business days and required handoffs across multiple teams was rebuilt in under a week. The same process now completes in fifteen minutes with zero human handoffs.

Engineers participating in a multi-day "Flashbuild" sprint delivered production features in two days — a pace that would have been unimaginable twelve months ago. The engagement marks a turning point in what enterprise AI adoption can look like when it is not treated as a side project.

At Natera, the story is equally striking. Engineers working on FDA-regulated clinical genomics software have adopted Claude at a rate that has surprised even Liatrio's own practitioners. One engineer ran a 14-step clinical workflow dry-test without being asked. Another extended the pipeline to her own HCLS project within days.

Azure Provisioning Time Plummets From Five Business Days to Fifteen Minutes
Boeing Digital Services engineers stunned as Claude-powered workflow eliminates every manual handoff; 'Flashbuild' delivers production features in 48 hours
By the Liatrio Data Desk  |  Boeing Digital Services

When Liatrio first arrived at Boeing Digital Services, the engineers tasked with cloud infrastructure provisioning had grown accustomed to a familiar rhythm: submit a request, wait five business days, navigate handoffs between multiple teams, and hope nothing fell through the cracks. It was a process that had calcified over years into something nobody questioned.

Liatrio questioned it. And then, using Claude Sonnet and Claude Opus deployed via Azure AI Foundry, they rebuilt it.

The new workflow runs in fifteen minutes. It requires zero human handoffs. And it was built in under a week — a timeline that Boeing engineers initially refused to believe was possible.

"The same process now runs in 15 minutes with zero handoffs," one Liatrio practitioner confirmed. "That's not an improvement. That's a different category of outcome."

The engine behind the transformation is Spec-Driven Development, a methodology Liatrio has refined over its engagements and that Claude is uniquely suited to execute. Engineers write specifications; Claude generates, reviews, and iterates on the implementation. The feedback loop collapses from days to minutes.

"A workflow that took 5 business days and multiple team handoffs was rebuilt in under a week. The same process now runs in 15 minutes — zero handoffs."

The Flashbuild sprint deepened the story. Two product teams and one platform team worked alongside Liatrio practitioners in an intensive, time-boxed delivery exercise. The result: production features delivered in two days. The measured pace improvement against the Boeing baseline was eight times faster. The experience was described by participants as "a different way of working," not a faster version of the old one.

Healthcare & Life Sciences
Natera Engineers Declare: "I Didn't Write a Single Line of Code"
FDA-regulated clinical software teams embrace Claude at unprecedented pace; organic adoption spreads beyond prescribed workflows across genomics and diagnostics
By the Liatrio Health Sciences Correspondent

There is a particular phrase that has been circulating among Natera's engineering teams with increasing frequency over the past several months. It is not the kind of phrase that appears in project plans or status reports. It is the kind of phrase that surfaces in hallway conversations and post-sprint retrospectives: "I didn't write a single line of code."

The engineer who first said it was not exaggerating. Working within Natera's QCI team on FDA-regulated clinical genomics software, she had spent a full day delivering features — real features, tested features, mergeable features — without touching a keyboard for code entry. Claude had done it. She had directed it.

Liatrio embedded Claude Code into the QCI team's development environment alongside OpenTelemetry instrumentation and Spec-Driven Development. The initial scope covered four regulated repositories. The results were immediate. Within days, an engineer ran a 14-step clinical workflow dry-test entirely unprompted — not because he was told to, but because he was curious whether it would work. It did.

The organic spread of adoption is what distinguishes the Natera engagement from a standard deployment. Another engineer, working on a separate project, extended the Liatrio-built pipeline to her own HCLS work within days of observing it on the QCI team. She did not ask for permission. She did not file a request. She simply used it.

This is the signal Liatrio watches for: the moment when a new capability propagates laterally through an organization without mandate. It is the surest sign that something has genuinely changed, rather than been imposed.

The scaling roadmap at Natera is ambitious. The engagement began with one team. It is now expanding to 80+ teams across genomics and diagnostics — an organization-wide transformation that requires not just tooling but culture, training, and a new understanding of what software delivery means in a regulated environment.

Five strategic plays define the Natera program: FDA and MasterControl compliance automation, enterprise release intelligence, a clinical genomics co-pilot, company-wide AI fluency development, and full agentic SDLC orchestration. Each represents a distinct value layer; together they constitute a platform for how Natera will build software for the next decade.

Automated MR review pipelines now catch issues that previously required senior engineer time. Release intelligence surfaces patterns across the codebase that were previously invisible. The QCI team's velocity has measurably increased — but the more important change is qualitative: engineers are spending their time thinking rather than typing.

The L1–L5 AI Fluency Framework governs how Natera tracks capability development across its engineering population. Every team member is assessed and placed; every engagement is designed to move people up the ladder.

Liatrio Builds Enterprise AI Certification Program in 72 Hours; Industry Experts Baffled
The Claude Certification Academy takes engineers from zero to productive in days — not months. Fifty engineers upskilling within the first month.
By the Liatrio Workforce Development Desk

The demand for engineers who can work effectively alongside AI has outrun the supply of programs that can teach it. Most enterprise training initiatives move at the speed of procurement: six months to design, three months to approve, another quarter to deploy. By the time engineers complete the program, the technology has changed.

Liatrio decided to try something different. When it became clear that the engagements at Boeing and Natera required a systematic approach to AI fluency — not just tool access, but genuine capability — the firm built the Claude Certification Academy in three days.

The CCA is built on Anthropic's open-source educational materials, adapted and extended for enterprise delivery contexts. It is not a generic AI literacy program. It is a practitioner's curriculum: opinionated, hands-on, and tightly integrated with the workflows that Liatrio's engineers actually use on engagement.

The Forge cohort program places engineers in structured cohorts where they complete the CCA curriculum while working on real delivery challenges. The experience is designed to be uncomfortable in the right ways: participants are expected to produce, not just learn.

In the program's first month, more than fifty engineers achieved certification. The pipeline is growing. Clients who previously struggled to articulate an AI upskilling strategy now point to their CCA cohort as evidence of organizational capability.

"Other partners will stand up Bedrock," one Liatrio principal observed. "We teach organizations how to actually transform. That's a different product."

The distinction matters because transformation requires more than access. Engineers need to know not just that AI tools exist, but how to structure the work that AI performs, how to review and validate AI outputs in regulated environments, and how to identify the workflows where AI creates the most leverage.
